Resume Guide

Personal Details

At the top of your resume, clearly put your full name alongside contact information. Always include an email and phone number. Depending on your field, add a LinkedIn, GitHub, or portfolio.

Education

As a student or recent graduate, your education is your primary qualification. Include GPA, relevant coursework (e.g. Data Structures, Thermodynamics), and graduation date.

Experience and Projects

Experience: Focus on transferable skills and measurable impact. Avoid listing responsibilities — show outcomes.

Projects: Highlight the skills, purpose, and results of academic or personal work.

Quantify Your Impact

Use numbers whenever possible. A strong formula is: “Accomplished X as measured by Y, by doing Z.”

Formatting and Other Key Elements

Keep your resume to one page, use consistent fonts, clean spacing, and bullet points. Avoid paragraphs.

ATS Optimization

Applicant Tracking Systems scan resumes for keywords. Use standard headings, avoid graphics, and mirror language from the job posting.

Common Resume Red Flags

Avoid words without evidence (don't just list skills without showing where you learned them), excessive coursework, and over-designed resumes with icons or graphics.

Cover Letters

Purpose

A cover letter explains why you want the role and why you’re a strong fit. Research the company’s mission, products, and future goals.

Recommended Structure

Paragraph 1: Why this role and company.
Paragraph 2: Discuss experiences and projects from your resume.
Paragraph 3: Confident closing.

Formatting

Keep your cover letter consistent with your resume in font, spacing, and structure.

Build a Narrative

Choose one or a few experiences/projects from your resume. Describe a challenge you faced in each, your actions to solve that problem, and the outcome. You should especially focus on how you solved the issue, and the skills you learned/used.

Common Mistakes

Avoid repeating your resume, being vague, using a casual tone, or claiming skills without explaining how you developed them.

Call to Action

End confidently and express interest in further discussion.

When You Should (and Shouldn’t) Write One

If optional, only submit a cover letter if you can tailor it to the role well. Generic letters may not be helpful.

Interview Tips

Before Your Interview

Research the company, role, team, and recent developments. Prepare questions regarding these things that you can ask during the interview. Wear formal clothes (whether your interview is in-person or online).

The STAR Method

Use this method when answering questions related to problem-solving or your past experiences. Situation, Task, Action, Result. Focus on your contributions.

Ask Your Own Questions

Use the questions you made before the interview (and any others that may have come up during the interview) to ask once your interview is complete.

After the Interview

Send a thank-you email within 24 hours referencing the conversation.

Virtual Interview Setup

Test your setup, ensure good lighting, and choose a clean, distraction-free background.
